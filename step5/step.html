5.Finding Annulo- Papillary distance

Knowing the precise location of papillary muscles is very important in implanting Avatar due to its chorded nature. So, first specifying the annulo-papillary distance becomes the major step in this implantation planning.
Annulo- papillary distance is the distance from the centre of the mitral valve’s annulus to the tip of the papillary muscle. To find the two extremes of the annulo-papillary distances, we will be using the end systole and end diastole stages instead of using all 0-100% cycle phases.

Go to the end-systole stage.

Use marker feature under measurement on the right side of the page in 3Mensio.

Rotate the view slowly to find the tip of each papillary muscle, one at a time.

Right clicking on the marker will let you edit the name, size and colour of the marker.

Pick different colours and name the anterolateral muscle “APM” and the posteromedial one “PPM”. Note that the APM is the one closer to the septum when viewed from the side .

Place the marker point at the centre of the annulus. Edit it to make it bigger than the marker size of APM and PPM.

Rotate to ensure the marker remains in the exact same spot from every angle. It should align perfectly with the white centre point of the annulus.

When you rotate, at one view, you will see that the centre is off

The blue centre point must be always following the white annulus centre.

Right click anywhere within the bottom right view and hover over the ‘measurements” then choose “Distance(between markers)”.

Click on the markers or the captions of the markers then the distance will be shown. Repeat the same steps for PPM as well.

Go to the end-diastole stage. And find the distances between the annulus to the APM and PPM by repeating the same steps.

CAUTION:

The limitation with 3Mensio software is that it does not allow for the creation of planes within the workspace. So, the only option is to virtually reference the annulus plane. To reference the slanting plane, the trigones become crucial features. We can assume that the virtual plane we are envisioning aligns with the line formed by the center point and the two trigones.

Getting X,Y,Z coordinates


Now, you have got annulo-papillary distances. These distances are for you to estimate how far the muscles are from the centre of the annulus.But these dimensions alone are not enough for getting the precise location of the papillary muscle when doing the open heart surgery and viewing the mitral valve from the top.To visualise the movement of the valve throughout the whole cardiac cycle, the precise x,y,z coordinates are needed. To find the x,y,z distances of each muscle from the centre, we will be setting the annulus plane as the reference.
Note : Make sure you have located trigones and turned on visibility for mitral annulus ring. Leave the APM, PPM and centre markers as they were. To see the distances and angles measurements clearly, we will be using the top right side of the 3Mensio workspace this time.

X,Y,Z coordinates


To visualise the annulus plane and set the centre of the annulus as the origin point, we need to create four more marker points. In this case, we will name it “S” and “R”, assuming all points are perpendicular to each other.

For finding the x,y,z distances, we must always be envisioning the annulus plane although it is not visible in 3Mensio.


APM to annulus centre


First, create a white marker near the annulus plane. Right click, measurement, Angle (between markers).

Click on the APM, white marker “S” and then the centre point. An angle will be formed. Adjust the angle until it shows 90 degrees.

Important to note that we are working on a 3-dimensional workspace and there can be many locations where the angle formed is 90 degrees.

So, aligning that point “S” with the annulus plane becomes very important. Rotate the view and see if “S” stays more or less on the annulus plane.

Measure the distance between APM and “S”. This will be the Z distance from the annulus.

Create a blue marker near point “R”.Right click , measurement , Angle (between markers).

Click on “S”, “R” and then the centre point . An angle will be formed. Adjust the angle until it shows 90 degrees.

When adjusting the angle, it is important to rotate around and see if the triangle shaped formed is lying flat on the plane. Even after getting a 90 degree angle, you still have to rotate around and see if the trigones, centre point and the triangle formed all lied about the same plane that we are envisioning.

After setting all four markers, rotate around and see if all the points somewhat lie on the plane.

Measure the distance between “R” and “S”. This will be the Y distance from the annulus.

Measure the distance between “R” and the centre. This will be the X distance from the annulus.
